Theory to Practice

During my practicum, I applied educational theories to guide lesson design, teaching strategies, and student engagement in ICT and Python lessons. The following three models/theories informed my approach and were implemented in practical classroom activities.

1Ô∏è‚É£ Constructivist Learning Theory (Piaget & Vygotsky)

Constructivism emphasizes learners actively constructing their knowledge through experience. Piaget focuses on developmental stages, while Vygotsky highlights social interaction and scaffolding.

  • Students experimented with Python code, predicting outputs and debugging errors independently.
  • Pairs designed Flowcharts and presented their solutions to the class.
  • I provided guided hints and scaffolding rather than direct answers.
Lesson Plan: Python

Applying Constructivist strategies encouraged active learning, collaboration, and independent problem-solving.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Bloom‚Äôs Taxonomy (Cognitive Domain)

Bloom‚Äôs Taxonomy categorizes cognitive skills from lower-order to higher-order thinking, helping structure lessons from simple comprehension to creation.

  • Introduced basic Python concepts for understanding and remembering.
  • Students debugged code (analyze) and created small programs (create).
  • Exit tickets and reflection questions assessed higher-order thinking skills.
Lesson Plan: Programming

Using Bloom‚Äôs Taxonomy ensured lessons gradually increased cognitive complexity and promoted higher-order thinking.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Experiential Learning (Kolb)

Kolb‚Äôs Experiential Learning Cycle emphasizes learning through experience, reflection, conceptualization, and experimentation.

  • Students actively coded exercises, then reflected on mistakes as a class.
  • Flowchart exercises involved hands-on creation followed by peer review and discussion.
  • Revisions encouraged experimentation and application of improvements.
Lesson PPT

Experiential learning allowed students to learn by doing, reflect on experiences, and iteratively improve their understanding.
